The Visionary

"There is no 'Planet B.' This is our home."

— Dr. Dieter Gruen, age 102

1922

Born in Walldorf, Germany. Into a Jewish family. His father was a school principal; his childhood was, by his own account, idyllic.

1936

Fled Nazi Germany alone at age 14. Hitler Youth beat him on his way home from school. His parents were sent to Buchenwald. He sailed past the Statue of Liberty on the HMS Georgic and settled in Little Rock, Arkansas.

1944

Recruited to the Manhattan Project at age 21. The only Northwestern graduate sent directly to Oak Ridge. Worked on electromagnetic separation of uranium-235 — helped produce ~60 kg of U-235 for the Hiroshima bomb.

"We must not allow nuclear weapons ever to be used again."

1947

Joined Argonne National Laboratory. Would remain for 65 years — one of the longest tenures in the lab's history. Solved the hafnium-zirconium separation that made nuclear submarines possible.

1958

U.S. Delegate, United Nations Conference on Peaceful Uses of Atomic Energy. Representing the nation at the highest level of international scientific diplomacy.

~1992

Invented Ultrananocrystalline Diamond (UNCD) — at approximately age 70. When most scientists retire, he created a revolutionary new form of diamond. Won the MRS Medal. Now used in artificial retinas, cardiac pacemakers, and dental implants.

2012

Conceived the graphene solar cell architecture — at age 90. Graphene shells surrounding ZnO nanowire cores. A radically new approach to photovoltaic conversion that could reach 85% theoretical efficiency.

2020

Guest at the State of the Union. Most recent patent at age 98. Nominated for the Presidential Medal of Freedom by a bipartisan coalition. Honored twice in the Congressional Record.

Today

Still in the laboratory at age 102. Featured in the documentary Change: A Warming Warning (2025). Still pushing the boundaries of what graphene-based solar can achieve.

"I count every day I'm able to do this. It won't last forever, but I'll do it as long as I can."

Deep Dive

The attosecond advantage.

Architecture

ZnO nanowires serve as the semiconductor core, wrapped in a graphene shell that acts simultaneously as optical antenna and rectifier. The 2.8 eV built-in potential at the graphene/ZnO junction — nearly 4× silicon's 0.7V — enables voltage outputs impossible with conventional architectures.

Built-in Potential: 2.8 eV → 3.5V demonstrated

Charge Transfer

Graphene's unique electronic structure enables ballistic charge transfer in attoseconds (10⁻¹⁸ seconds) — 10,000× faster than the femtosecond (10⁻¹⁵s) processes in silicon. Electrons are extracted before thermalization, capturing energy that silicon cells lose as waste heat.

Attosecond (10⁻¹⁸s) vs. Femtosecond (10⁻¹⁵s)

Linear Scaling

Photocurrent in the graphene/ZnO system scales linearly over 3 orders of magnitude of illumination intensity — from laboratory conditions to concentrated solar. Sheet resistance has been reduced 100× through process optimization.

Linear response: 1× to 1,000× illumination
